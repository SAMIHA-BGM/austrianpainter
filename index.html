Origin : From Craddle to Chaos

Born on April 20, 1889, in a quiet Austrian town that gives serious Ohio energy — no one knew this mid baby would cook up global DLC-level chaos. No glitches, no grand prophecy. Just lagging vibes and fate bugging out.

His dad, Alois, was the OG gooner — strict, mad controlling, walking red flag. His mom, Klara? An absolute NPC angel. She soft-launched Hitler's only ounce of humanity. (Literally his last W.)

Young Adolf just wanted to be an Artist. Like, fr. He thought he was HIM with the paintbrush. But Vienna’s art school said “skibidi no” — twice. No rizz, no portfolio, no future.

Instead of slaying canvases, he started slaying… well… you know how that turned out.

Bro got denied an art degree and said, “Fine. I’ll speedrun world domination.”

Maybe if someone had just bought one of his mid watercolor postcards, we wouldn’t have had a whole war sequel.

But alas. Vienna fumbled. And the villain arc began.

The Rise :

"The man learned HOW to speak, Before he KNEW how to Lead"

He didn’t march into power.
He talked his way in.

Twice denied by Vienna’s art school, Hitler’s dream of becoming a painter collapsed. He lived in shelters, selling postcards — poor, angry, forgotten. Then came World War I. He enlisted, served bravely, and was wounded. But what hurt more was Germany’s defeat. He didn’t see it as loss — he saw it as betrayal.

In 1919, still aimless gooner, he joined a tiny political group: the German Workers’ Party. When he spoke, people stopped to listen. He had passion, conviction — and hate. Within a year, he was leading it. They renamed it the Nazi Party.
Then came his first move for power: the Beer Hall Putsch in 1923. It failed. He was arrested.

But jail gave him time — and a pen.
There, he wrote Mein Kampf — a toxic mix of racism, revenge, and vision. Few took it seriously. They should have.
Once released, he rebuilt the party. Slowly, methodically. Then the Great Depression hit. Chaos spread. People were desperate. And Hitler offered answers — loud, simple, dangerous ones.

By 1933, Hitler wasn’t some radical on the fringe. He was Chancellor of Germany. Legally. Publicly. Applauded.

He didn’t steal power.

He was handed it.

Führungswille

Führungswille — the will to lead — became his doctrine, his drug, his destiny.
Power didn’t change him. It revealed him. The moment he became Chancellor, the theatre turned real. The masks came off.
He silenced opposition beneath the smoke of the Reichstag Fire, then sealed it with the Enabling Act. Parliament remained, yes — but it now spoke in unison, echoing only him.

The Gestapo arrived like whispers in the dark. Fear became law. Dissent became death. Jews, communists, the disabled, journalists — anyone inconvenient was rebranded as infection. And the people applauded as the cure spread.

He rewrote childhood. Mein Kampf became scripture. Swastikas replaced lullabies. Teachers became priests of ideology. The Hitler Youth did not march for truth — they marched for loyalty.

Factories roared. Highways stretched like veins through a nation reborn. On the surface, Germany stood tall again. But beneath — the soil was salted. And we, the living, stood in the shadow of a man who did not take power. He absorbed it. And demanded more.

“Obedience is the cornerstone of power.”

Hate

Racism turned into policy, genocide mapped on paper.

In the early 1930s, a series of administrative decisions and legal frameworks were enacted, gradually shaping a state-led definition of racial belonging. Official records documented population classifications, employment eligibility, and property rights based on ancestry.

1935

The Nuremberg Laws were introduced. They defined citizenship not by nationality, but by racial lineage. Marriages and relations between Jewish and non-Jewish Germans were prohibited.

1938

State decrees ordered the registration and expropriation of Jewish-owned businesses. The Kristallnacht event marked a visible shift — from institutional to physical destruction.

1942

Bureaucratic documentation from the Wannsee Conference outlines logistical coordination for the Final Solution — a term used to describe the mass deportation and extermination of targeted populations.

Fall

Paranoia, poison, collapse — no escape from consequence.

As Allied forces encircled Berlin in 1945, what was once strategic confidence gave way to fragmented communications, urgent bunker meetings, and irreversible decisions made in isolation.

April 20, 1945

The final public appearance — birthday greetings among ruins. The city was already under siege, yet the command structure persisted with a carefully curated image.

April 28, 1945

Reports of betrayal circulated. Telegrams, distorted facts, and rumors became indistinguishable. Trust among allies fractured — internal collapse had begun before the external one completed.

April 30, 1945

In a concrete room beneath the Reich Chancellery, the final act unfolded: a pair of cyanide capsules, a pistol, and a decision written in ink and sealed in envelopes. Silence followed.

“History is written by the survivors. Silence is written by the defeated.”

After the collapse, fragments of ideology remained — digitized, distorted, or denied. Echoes now exist in books, forums, memes, censorship wars.

The internet reanimated the past in unpredictable ways. Documents once sealed are searchable. Symbols reappear as avatars and provocations.

Different nations handled the past differently — museums, erased monuments, diverging textbooks. Remembrance clashed with revision.

The past remains taboo — and fascination. Banned books trend. Documentaries go viral. Subcultures flirt with boundaries of memory, law, and taste.

“The past is never dead. It’s not even past.” — William Faulkner
